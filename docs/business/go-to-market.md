# Go-to-Market Strategy

## Beachhead: one corridor, not a global launch

The temptation in cross-border payments is to claim broad country coverage from day one. The defensible version is narrower: **launch in the corridor with the strongest evidence already gathered, prove it works, then expand corridor by corridor.**

Based on the research already done in this project (see [Potential Partners](potential-partners.md) and [Alternative Approaches](alternative-approaches.md)), Year 1 targets the largest freelancer market in each of three priority regions: **Nigeria** (Africa), **India** (Asia), and **Brazil** (Latin America). All three are corridors the systemic alternatives (UPI-PayNow, Project Nexus, ASEAN mesh) do not reach at the retail freelancer level, since they require a domestic instant-payment system on the receiving end that most of these markets don't yet have linked internationally the way Southeast Asia does.

## Launch sequence

| Phase | Focus | Exit criterion |
|---|---|---|
| 1. Three parallel corridors | One sending country (EU, via SEPA) to Nigeria, India, and Brazil, one payout partner each | A real transfer settles in each corridor, payout confirmed, compliance anchor verifiable on HashScan |
| 2. Corridor depth | Add 2-3 more receiving corridors per region using the same sending-side rail | Partner agreements signed, not new engineering |
| 3. Second sending market | Add a second sending country/currency (e.g. UK, GBP) | Proves the model isn't EUR-specific |
| 4. Second target segment | Expand from freelancer remittances to general diaspora remittances (the second target market named in [Problem Analysis](../get-started/problem-analysis.md)) | Volume from segment 2 exceeds a defined threshold relative to segment 1 |

Each phase is gated on **partner agreements**, not engineering work. Consistent with the scalability analysis in [Business Model](business-model.md): this is a commercially-constrained business, not a technically-constrained one.

## Customer acquisition

| Channel | Why it fits this specific segment |
|---|---|
| Freelance platform partnerships | Freelancers already aggregate on platforms; a payout-method integration reaches thousands of users through one partnership, not one user at a time |
| Diaspora community channels | WhatsApp groups, community associations, and remittance comparison sites are where diaspora senders already research options, not general advertising |
| Partner co-marketing | Licensed payout partners (Yellow Card, MTN MoMo, and equivalents in India/Brazil) have existing user bases; a co-marketing agreement reaches recipients' senders indirectly |

**What this explicitly avoids:** paid general-audience advertising as a first channel. Cross-border remittance senders are price- and trust-sensitive; acquisition in this space works through existing trusted channels (community, platform, partner), not broad awareness campaigns, especially pre-revenue.

## Pricing at launch

Per [Business Model](business-model.md): a transparent flat fee plus a stated FX spread, meaningfully below the global average cost, undercutting the *hidden* spread most incumbents charge, not necessarily the *total* cost, since the licensed partners' margins are the majority of the cost regardless of who orchestrates the transfer. The defensible pricing claim at launch is transparency and a real, moderate discount, not being unconditionally cheaper. (The exact discount figure and full unit economics are still being finalized separately.)

## What would make this fail, stated honestly

Per the same section's own risk framing: a licensed CASP (Due, BVNK) adding a compliance-anchoring layer of their own would remove this project's main differentiation quickly, since they already have the licensing and corridor coverage. The go-to-market bet is that they are optimizing for corridors with existing volume, leaving the corridors this project targets under-served for long enough to establish a partner relationship advantage.
